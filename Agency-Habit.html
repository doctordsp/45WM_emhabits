Mental Habit: Action-Orientation and Agency in Engineering Education

1. Description and Theoretical Framework

Definition and Importance

Action-orientation and agency is a mental habit characterized by a proactive stance toward problem-solving, an internalized locus of control, and the ability to find or create resources to overcome obstacles. This habit involves taking initiative without waiting for directive, seeing oneself as capable of effecting change, and demonstrating resourcefulness when faced with limitations or constraints.

For engineers, developing a strong sense of agency is critical because engineering work frequently involves navigating complex, ambiguous challenges with incomplete information and limited resources. As noted by researchers, engineers with an internal locus of control “tend to set more challenging goals, persist in the face of adversity, experience less job stress, and be more successful generally” (Cobb-Clark, 2015). The habit of action-orientation enables engineers to move beyond analysis paralysis and take decisive steps to address problems, even when perfect solutions aren’t immediately apparent.

Theoretical Framework: The Agency-Resource-Action Model

The development of action-orientation and agency can be conceptualized through a three-component model that addresses the psychological, resource-based, and behavioral aspects of this mental habit:

  1. Agency Component (Psychological): This involves developing an internal locus of control and self-efficacy:

    • Internal Locus of Control: The belief that one has significant control over outcomes rather than being at the mercy of external forces or luck
    • Self-Efficacy: Confidence in one’s ability to execute actions necessary to achieve desired outcomes
    • Growth Mindset: The belief that abilities can be developed through dedication and hard work

  2. Resource Component (Strategic): This involves developing the capability to identify, obtain, or create necessary resources:

    • Resource Awareness: Recognizing what resources are available and how they might be leveraged
    • Resource Acquisition: Skills in obtaining additional resources through networking, collaboration, or persuasion
    • Resource Creation: The ability to develop new resources or repurpose existing ones in novel ways

  3. Action Component (Behavioral): This involves translating agency and resources into tangible action:

    • Initiative: Taking action without being prompted or directed by others
    • Persistence: Maintaining effort despite obstacles or setbacks
    • Adaptability: Adjusting approach based on feedback and changing conditions

This model recognizes that action-orientation and agency in engineering is not merely about positive thinking, but involves a complex interplay between psychological factors, strategic resource management, and behavioral habits that can be systematically developed through engineering education.

2. Engineering Education Applications

Detailed Implementation: The “Constrained Resources Engineering Challenge”

Overview

The Constrained Resources Engineering Challenge is a semester-long project-based learning experience designed for second-year mechanical engineering students. The challenge develops action-orientation and agency by requiring students to design and build a functional mechanical system with deliberately limited resources, minimal instruction, and progressively increasing constraints. The experience forces students to develop resourcefulness, take initiative, and build confidence in their ability to overcome challenging limitations.

Technical Setup and Materials

Initial Resources (Phase 1):

  • Basic mechanical components kit (gears, pulleys, shafts, bearings, etc.)
  • Standard set of hand tools
  • Limited selection of materials (aluminum, wood, plastics)
  • Basic fabrication equipment access (drill press, band saw, basic machine shop)
  • Technical documentation for components
  • Modest budget ($50 per team)

Progressive Constraint Elements:

  • Resource reduction schedule (components and materials reduced by 25% in Phase 2)
  • Time constraints that tighten as project progresses
  • “Disruption cards” introducing unexpected limitations
  • “Resource scarcity” periods where certain materials become unavailable
  • Collaborative restriction phases where teams cannot communicate
  • “Market volatility” where resource costs fluctuate unpredictably

Assessment Tools:

  • Engineering journals documenting decision-making processes
  • Resource utilization tracking forms
  • Team retrospective templates
  • Initiative assessment rubrics
  • Self and peer evaluation forms
  • Process observation protocols for instructors

Implementation Process

Phase 1: Initial Challenge and Baseline Assessment (3 weeks)

  1. Students form teams of 3-4 members and are presented with the semester challenge: Design and build a mechanical system that can transport a small payload up a variable incline with specified performance metrics.

  2. Teams receive their initial resource allocation and modest budget.

  3. Importantly, instructors provide minimal procedural guidance, offering only:

    • Technical performance requirements
    • Safety boundaries
    • Basic schedule milestones
    • Evaluation criteria

  4. Teams must develop their own:

    • Design process documentation
    • Task allocation systems
    • Decision-making frameworks
    • Testing protocols

  5. During this phase, instructors assess baseline action-orientation traits:

    • How quickly teams begin active work versus planning
    • Whether teams ask for permission or forgiveness
    • Who takes initiative without being assigned roles
    • How teams respond to initial obstacles

Phase 2: Resource Constraints and Adaptation (4 weeks)

  1. Once initial designs are developed, the instructor introduces the first major constraint:

    • 25% reduction in available materials and components
    • Introduction of “disruption cards” that randomly remove a critical resource
    • No additional budget allocations permitted

  2. Teams must adapt their designs while maintaining required performance metrics, forcing:

    • Creative repurposing of existing materials
    • Development of alternative approaches
    • Strategic decisions about what elements to preserve versus modify

  3. Weekly team debriefs focus on:

    • How constraints affect team dynamics
    • Individual response to frustration
    • Development of creative workarounds
    • Shifts in locus of control perceptions

  4. Instructors monitor and document:

    • Which teams proactively adapt versus requesting exceptions
    • How quickly teams pivot to new approaches
    • Evidence of resourcefulness in material usage
    • Signs of initiative in finding alternative solutions

Phase 3: Market and Collaboration Constraints (4 weeks)

  1. The instructor introduces complex systemic constraints:

    • “Market volatility” creates unpredictable resource pricing
    • Temporary “resource shortages” make certain components unavailable
    • Communication blackout periods limit team collaboration time
    • Random “inspection challenges” require immediate demonstration of progress

  2. Teams must now not only adapt their technical solutions but also:

    • Develop contingency plans for resource scarcity
    • Create decentralized decision-making processes
    • Implement robust documentation for asynchronous work
    • Build resilience into their designs and processes

  3. Weekly reflection sessions specifically address:

    • Team response to uncertainty and ambiguity
    • Individual comfort with autonomous decision-making
    • Development of proactive versus reactive mindsets
    • Emerging leadership and initiative patterns

Phase 4: Final Challenge and Meta-Reflection (3 weeks)

  1. For the final phase, teams face a culminating challenge:

    • 24-hour notice to integrate an entirely new requirement into their design
    • Reduction of remaining materials budget by 50%
    • Requirement to document their process in a format usable by future students

  2. Teams must deliver:

    • A functional prototype meeting all performance requirements
    • Complete documentation of their engineering process
    • Analysis of resource utilization efficiency
    • Reflection on their growth in action-orientation and agency

  3. The final assessment includes:

    • Technical performance of the prototype
    • Resource efficiency metrics
    • Process documentation quality
    • Team and individual growth in agency metrics
    • Peer and self-assessment of action-orientation development

Learning Outcomes and Habit Development

This implementation develops several key engineering competencies:

  1. Design under constraint: Students learn to create solutions with limited resources
  2. Adaptive problem-solving: Students develop the ability to pivot when initial approaches fail
  3. Resource optimization: Students learn to maximize the utility of limited materials
  4. Process resilience: Students build systems that can withstand unexpected disruptions

Most importantly, the experience systematically cultivates action-orientation and agency by:

  1. Creating an environment where passive approaches consistently fail
  2. Requiring students to take initiative without clear instructions
  3. Building confidence through progressive mastery experiences
  4. Developing resourcefulness as a normalized response to constraints
  5. Reinforcing an internal locus of control through repeated demonstration of agency

Assessment data from implementations of similar challenges shows that students initially display frustration and seek additional resources or clearer guidance, but gradually develop greater comfort with ambiguity and constraints. By the end of the semester, most teams demonstrate significantly higher levels of initiative, resourcefulness, and confidence in their ability to overcome engineering challenges with limited resources.

3. Assessment and Measurement

Indicators of Action-Orientation and Agency as a Mental Habit

Observable behaviors that indicate action-orientation and agency has become habitual in engineering students include:

  • Initiating problem-solving without waiting for instruction or permission
  • Persisting through obstacles rather than becoming discouraged
  • Seeking creative ways to overcome resource limitations
  • Taking responsibility for outcomes rather than blaming circumstances
  • Expressing confidence in ability to affect change
  • Demonstrating comfort with ambiguity and incomplete information
  • Actively seeking feedback rather than waiting for evaluation
  • Setting ambitious goals beyond minimum requirements
  • Creating contingency plans for potential obstacles
  • Offering solutions rather than just identifying problems

Assessment Methods

1. Locus of Control Instruments Validated psychological measures like Rotter’s Internal-External Locus of Control Scale can be modified for engineering contexts to assess whether students attribute outcomes to their own actions versus external factors (Cobb-Clark, 2015).

2. Behavioral Observation Protocols Structured observation of student behaviors during open-ended projects can track specific indicators of initiative, persistence, and resourcefulness as they develop over time (Edutopia, 2015).

3. Resource Utilization Metrics Tracking how students use available resources and their strategies for overcoming resource limitations provides objective measures of their resourcefulness development (Indeed, 2024).

4. Decision Analysis Analyzing the decision-making processes documented in engineering journals, with particular attention to how students respond to constraints and setbacks, can reveal growth in action-orientation (Zippia, 2023).

5. Initiative Mapping Documenting when and how students take action without prompting across multiple projects over time can demonstrate the development of initiative as a habitual response (SkillsCamp, 2024).

6. Self and Peer Reflection Structured reflection exercises that ask students to assess their own and their peers’ action-orientation provides valuable insight into the development of these habits from multiple perspectives (Arizona State University, n.d.).

4. Challenges and Considerations

Potential Barriers

1. Educational Conditioning Many students have been conditioned through previous educational experiences to wait for instruction, follow prescribed processes, and avoid risk, making the development of action-orientation challenging (Education Hub, 2024).

2. Fear of Failure Students may be reluctant to take initiative or try creative approaches due to concerns about grades, peer judgment, or damage to their academic standing if they fail (Towards Data Science, 2025).

3. Structural Constraints Traditional engineering curriculum structures with rigid assignments, clear instructions, and prescribed outcomes may inadvertently reinforce passive learning approaches (Frontiers, 2020).

4. Assessment Misalignment Traditional assessment methods often evaluate technical knowledge rather than process skills like initiative and resourcefulness, potentially sending mixed messages about what is valued (Science Direct, 2019).

5. Individual Differences Students begin with different baseline tendencies toward internal or external locus of control based on prior experiences and cultural influences (Wikipedia, 2024).

Mitigation Strategies

1. Progressive Challenge Design Scaffolding challenges with gradually increasing ambiguity and decreasing guidance helps students build confidence in their autonomous problem-solving capabilities (Bright Hub, 2010).

2. Explicit Value Signaling Clearly communicating the value of action-orientation and agency in engineering careers and incorporating these traits into formal assessment helps students understand their importance (Leader in Me, 2019).

3. Failure Reframing Creating a learning environment where failed attempts are valued as learning opportunities rather than mistakes reduces the perceived risk of taking initiative (Matter App, n.d.).

4. Faculty Development Training faculty to resist the urge to provide too much guidance when students are struggling helps maintain appropriate challenge levels for agency development (Canadian Association of Second Language Teachers, 2021).

5. Success Story Highlighting Sharing examples of how resourcefulness and action-orientation have led to engineering innovations helps students recognize the value of these traits (SkillsCamp, 2024).
